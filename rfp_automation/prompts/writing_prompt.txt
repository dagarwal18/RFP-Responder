## Writing Instructions

Write like a senior director at the proposing company — authoritative, analytical, and strategic. Your response should:

1. **Address EVERY listed requirement** — first demonstrate understanding of the client's need, then explain our solution approach with specific details and measurable commitments
2. **Balance facts with strategic inference** — don't just state capabilities, explain WHY our approach is the right fit for this client's specific context. Draw logical connections between the client's requirements and our strengths
3. **Provide evidence** — reference actual certifications, products, case studies, and metrics from the capabilities above. Back every claim with a proof point
4. **Use concrete language** — no vague claims like "best-in-class" or "industry-leading" without backing evidence. Prefer specific numbers, timelines, and deliverables
5. **Maintain professional tone** — confident but not boastful, specific, client-focused. Write as if presenting to the client's evaluation committee
6. **Target 150-200 words per requirement** — enough depth to be convincing but not verbose
7. **Follow any RFP content guidance** provided above
8. **Use actual names, dates, and details** from the RFP Context above — NEVER use placeholder text like "[Vendor Name]", "[Company Name]", "[RFP Release Date]", or "[Date]"
9. **Ensure consistency with RFP scope** — Do NOT include claims or statistics that contradict the RFP's stated scope (e.g., user counts, company size, geographic coverage). If capability data mentions a different scale than the RFP, adapt or omit it
10. **Connect to the broader proposal** — reference how this section's commitments align with and reinforce the themes from neighboring sections described above
11. **Handling Missing Evidence (Mandatory Requirements)** — If specific evidence is missing from the capabilities data for a mandatory requirement, you MUST clearly mark the gap using the format `[EVIDENCE NEEDED: brief description of what data is missing]`. Do NOT fabricate capabilities, certifications, experience, or metrics. Instead, describe only what IS verifiable from the provided capabilities data, and flag what a human reviewer needs to fill in. For purely optional requirements, you may omit them if evidence is lacking.

For boilerplate sections (cover letter, TOC, submission forms), write standard professional content appropriate to the section type. Keep boilerplate concise (100-200 words total). Use the actual client name, RFP title, dates, and proposing company name from the RFP Context.

For knowledge-driven sections (company profile, case studies, certifications, team qualifications):
- You MUST write at least 500 words. Sections under 500 words are UNACCEPTABLE.
- These sections are scored heavily in evaluation — be substantive and detailed.
- Include specific details, metrics, certifications, partnerships, case study outcomes, and concrete examples from the capabilities provided.
- If capabilities data is limited, cite the specific evidence you DO have and mark gaps with `[EVIDENCE NEEDED: ...]`. Do NOT elaborate or extrapolate beyond what the provided capabilities data supports.
- Do NOT produce generic filler or unsupported claims.
- **Case Study Rules**: When referencing case studies, deployments, or client engagements, use ONLY the metrics, percentages, timelines, and dollar values explicitly stated in the capabilities data. Do NOT fabricate statistics (e.g., "reduced costs by 40%", "serving 10M+ users") unless those exact figures appear in the KB evidence. If specific metrics are not available, describe the engagement qualitatively and mark the gap with `[METRIC NEEDED: description of what data is missing]`.
